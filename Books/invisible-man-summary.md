# Invisible Man

**Author:** Ralph Ellison  
**Published:** 1952  
**Genre:** Literary fiction, Bildungsroman, Social commentary

## Overview

*Invisible Man* follows an unnamed Black narrator from the Deep South to Harlem, exploring racial identity and social invisibility in mid-20th century America. The novel combines realistic social commentary with surrealist elements to examine how racism renders Black Americans "invisible" to white society while also exploring universal themes of identity and self-discovery.

## Plot Summary

### The College Years
The narrator, a model student at a Southern Black college, accidentally shows a white trustee (Mr. Norton) the seedy side of Black life, including the incestuous sharecropper Jim Trueblood and a bar full of mentally ill Black veterans. Dr. Bledsoe, the college president, expels him with sealed letters of "recommendation."

### Northern Disillusionment
In New York, the narrator discovers Bledsoe's letters actually prevent his employment. After an accident at a paint factory (Liberty Paints), he undergoes shock therapy that erases parts of his memory.

### The Brotherhood
The narrator joins the Brotherhood, a communist-like organization, becoming a spokesman in Harlem. He gains prominence but discovers the Brotherhood manipulates Black interests for their own agenda.

### Riots and Retreat
After the death of Tod Clifton (shot by police for selling Sambo dolls) and riots orchestrated by the Brotherhood, the narrator falls into a coal cellar. He decides to remain underground, stealing electricity and living invisibly while writing his story.

## Major Themes

### Invisibility
The central metaphor—being unseen by white society—extends to how stereotypes and expectations prevent genuine recognition of individual humanity.

### Identity and Self-Creation
The narrator tries on various identities (model student, worker, spokesman) before realizing he must define himself.

### Power and Manipulation
Every institution—the college, Liberty Paints, the Brotherhood—uses the narrator for their own purposes while claiming to help him.

### Race and Racism
The novel explores both Southern and Northern racism, showing how paternalism can be as destructive as outright hostility.

### History and Progress
Questions whether Black Americans can transcend history or remain trapped by it, symbolized by recurring references to running.

## Key Symbols

### The Briefcase
Given at his graduation, the narrator fills it with items representing his various identities, carrying the weight of others' expectations.

### The Paint Factory
Liberty Paints' slogan "Keep America Pure" and its "Optic White" paint mixed with black drops symbolize racial dynamics in America.

### The Sambo Doll
Tod Clifton's dancing doll represents racist stereotypes and the strings controlling Black Americans.

### Light and Electricity
The narrator's stolen electricity represents both enlightenment and subversion of the system.

### The Hole/Underground
His underground dwelling suggests both hiding and a vantage point for understanding society.

## Character Analysis

### The Narrator
Intelligent but naive, he slowly recognizes how others use him. His journey from blindness to sight drives the narrative.

### Dr. Bledsoe
The college president who maintains power by accommodating white expectations while ruthlessly controlling other Black people.

### Brother Jack
The Brotherhood's leader who literally has a glass eye, symbolizing his half-blind vision of racial issues.

### Ras the Exhorter/Destroyer
A Black nationalist who opposes the Brotherhood's integrationist approach, representing alternative responses to racism.

### Rinehart
A mysterious figure with multiple identities (pimp, preacher, gambler), showing the possibilities of invisibility.

### Mary Rambo
The maternal figure who shelters the narrator, representing authentic Black community values.

## Literary Techniques

### First-Person Retrospective
The narrator tells his story from underground, allowing for reflection and ironic distance.

### Surrealism and Symbolism
Dream sequences, the factory explosion, and the riot blend reality with symbolic meaning.

### Jazz Structure
The narrative improvises on themes like a jazz composition, with recurring motifs and variations.

### Allusions
References range from Booker T. Washington to Dostoevsky's *Notes from Underground*.

### Irony
Pervasive irony exposes the gap between American ideals and racial realities.

## Historical Context

### Post-WWII America
Black veterans returned to segregation after fighting for freedom abroad, highlighting American hypocrisy.

### Great Migration
The narrator's journey north reflects millions of Black Americans seeking opportunity outside the South.

### Communist Party and Black Americans
The Brotherhood mirrors the Communist Party's complicated relationship with Black liberation movements.

### Harlem Renaissance Aftermath
The novel both continues and critiques the Harlem Renaissance's exploration of Black identity.

## Key Episodes

### The Battle Royal
Young Black men fight blindfolded for white entertainment, establishing themes of manipulation and humiliation.

### The Eviction
The narrator's spontaneous speech during an elderly couple's eviction launches his career with the Brotherhood.

### The Factory Hospital
Electroshock therapy represents attempts to remake Black identity through technology and science.

### Clifton's Death
The shooting of Tod Clifton by a policeman precipitates the narrator's break with the Brotherhood.

## Philosophical Influences

### Existentialism
The narrator must create his own identity in an absurd world that denies his existence.

### Du Bois's Double Consciousness
The novel explores living with awareness of how white society sees you while maintaining your own self-perception.

### American Transcendentalism
Ironically invokes Emersonian self-reliance while showing its limits for Black Americans.

## Critical Reception

### Initial Response
Won the National Book Award in 1953, praised for psychological complexity and artistic achievement.

### Literary Significance
Elevated African American literature from protest fiction to modernist complexity.

### Ongoing Relevance
Continues to resonate as America grapples with racial visibility and identity.

## The Novel's Ending

The narrator's decision to emerge from underground suggests:
- Recognition that invisibility can be both refuge and trap
- Commitment to making himself visible on his own terms
- Understanding that his individual story has universal significance

## Legacy and Influence

*Invisible Man* revolutionized African American literature by combining modernist techniques with racial themes. Its influence extends beyond literature to discussions of:
- Racial identity and visibility
- The psychology of oppression
- Individual versus collective identity
- The complexity of Black experience in America

The novel refuses simple solutions or ideological answers, insisting on the full complexity of human identity. Its exploration of invisibility as both social condition and existential state continues to illuminate contemporary discussions of race, identity, and recognition. Ellison created a work that is simultaneously a powerful racial critique and a universal exploration of what it means to be human in a society that denies your humanity.
