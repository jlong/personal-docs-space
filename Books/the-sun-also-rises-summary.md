# The Sun Also Rises

**Author:** Ernest Hemingway  
**Published:** 1926  
**Genre:** Modernist fiction, Lost Generation literature

## Overview

*The Sun Also Rises* follows a group of American and British expatriates as they travel from Paris to Pamplona for the running of the bulls. The novel captures the disillusionment of the "Lost Generation" after World War I, exploring themes of masculinity, identity, and the search for meaning in a morally adrift world.

## Plot Summary

### Paris: The Expatriate Scene
Jake Barnes, an American journalist rendered impotent by a war wound, lives in Paris among fellow expatriates. He's in love with Lady Brett Ashley, a twice-divorced Englishwoman engaged to Mike Campbell. Their relationship is doomed by Jake's injury.

### The Circle of Friends
The group includes:
- Robert Cohn, a Jewish-American writer and former boxer
- Bill Gorton, Jake's friend and fishing companion
- Mike Campbell, Brett's alcoholic fiancé
- Count Mippipopolous, a wealthy Greek who understands "values"

### Journey to Spain
Jake and Bill travel to Spain for fishing and the fiesta. They're joined by Brett, Mike, and Cohn in Pamplona. Tensions explode as Brett has an affair with Pedro Romero, a young bullfighter.

### The Fiesta and Its Aftermath
Cohn, in love with Brett, attacks Jake and Mike when he discovers Brett's affair with Romero. The group dissolves in bitterness. Brett leaves with Romero but later summons Jake to Madrid, having sent the bullfighter away to preserve his career.

## Major Themes

### The Lost Generation
The characters embody post-war disillusionment, drinking heavily and drifting without purpose. Traditional values have been shattered by the war's meaningless carnage.

### Masculinity and Impotence
Jake's physical wound symbolizes the broader emasculation felt by war veterans. The novel explores various failed models of masculinity through Jake, Cohn, Mike, and Romero.

### Love and Sexuality
Jake and Brett's unconsummated love represents the impossibility of fulfillment in the post-war world. Brett's sexual freedom both liberates and destroys.

### Afición (Passion)
True passion, represented by bullfighting, offers a code of authentic living in a world otherwise devoid of meaning.

### Anti-Semitism and Alienation
Robert Cohn faces exclusion both as a Jew and as someone who doesn't understand the group's post-war cynicism.

## Key Symbols

### Bullfighting
Represents ritualized confrontation with death and a code of honor in contrast to the war's mechanized slaughter.

### The War Wound
Jake's injury symbolizes the invisible psychological wounds carried by the entire generation.

### Fishing
The peaceful interlude in Burguete represents temporary escape and authentic male friendship.

### The Fiesta
Both liberation and chaos, showing how the search for meaning through sensation ultimately fails.

## Character Analysis

### Jake Barnes
The narrator struggles with his love for Brett and his inability to consummate it. His stoicism masks deep pain, embodying Hemingway's "iceberg theory."

### Lady Brett Ashley
A "New Woman" who drinks, has multiple lovers, and wears her hair short. She's both liberated and trapped by her sexuality.

### Robert Cohn
An outsider who clings to romantic ideals the others have abandoned. His inability to adopt their cynicism makes him the group's scapegoat.

### Pedro Romero
At nineteen, he represents authentic masculinity and artistic integrity. His purity contrasts with the corruption of the expatriates.

### Mike Campbell
Brett's fiancé represents the bankrupt British aristocracy, drowning his inadequacy in alcohol and cruel humor.

## Hemingway's Style

### Iceberg Theory
Hemingway's minimalist prose suggests depths beneath the surface. What's unsaid is as important as what's stated.

### Dialogue
Characters speak in clipped, understated dialogue that reveals personality and conceals pain.

### Objective Narration
Jake rarely discusses his feelings directly, forcing readers to interpret his emotional state through actions and observations.

## The Code Hero

Hemingway's concept of living with "grace under pressure" is embodied by:
- Pedro Romero's dignity in the bullring
- Jake's stoic acceptance of his wound
- Count Mippipopolous's understanding of "values"

## Historical Context

### World War I's Impact
The novel captures the spiritual exhaustion following the war's unprecedented destruction and the collapse of traditional values.

### 1920s Paris
The expatriate community in Paris represented artistic freedom but also rootlessness and moral drift.

### The Spanish Fiesta
Hemingway's intimate knowledge of Spanish culture provides authenticity and a contrast to modern alienation.

## Literary Significance

### Modernist Technique
The novel's fragmented structure and understated emotion exemplify modernist literary techniques.

### Influence on American Literature
Hemingway's spare style influenced generations of writers and established new standards for prose economy.

### Cultural Impact
The novel popularized the term "Lost Generation" and became the defining portrait of 1920s expatriate life.

## Critical Reception

Initially controversial for its frank treatment of sexuality and drinking, the novel established Hemingway as a major voice. Critics have debated whether the novel ultimately affirms any positive values or remains purely nihilistic.

## Legacy

*The Sun Also Rises* endures as both a period piece capturing 1920s disillusionment and a timeless exploration of love, loss, and the search for authentic experience. Its influence extends beyond literature to shape cultural perceptions of the Lost Generation and the lasting impact of war on the human spirit. The novel's sparse prose and emotional restraint revolutionized American literary style while its themes of alienation and the search for meaning continue to resonate.
