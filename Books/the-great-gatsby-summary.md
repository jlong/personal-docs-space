# The Great Gatsby Summary

## Overview
**The Great Gatsby** is a novel by F. Scott Fitzgerald, published in 1925. Set in the Jazz Age of 1920s New York, it tells the story of Jay Gatsby's obsessive pursuit of his lost love, Daisy Buchanan. The novel is widely regarded as the greatest American novel and a masterpiece of 20th-century literature.

## Main Characters

- **Nick Carraway**: The narrator, a bond trader who moves to West Egg and becomes drawn into the drama surrounding Gatsby.
- **Jay Gatsby**: A mysterious, wealthy businessman obsessed with reclaiming his past love, Daisy. His real name is James Gatz.
- **Daisy Buchanan**: A beautiful, wealthy woman married to Tom. Gatsby's lost love and the object of his obsession.
- **Tom Buchanan**: Daisy's husband, a wealthy but brutish man from old money. Infidelities and arrogance define his character.
- **Jordan Baker**: Daisy's friend, a golf player and romantic interest of Nick. Dishonest and careless.
- **Myrtle Wilson**: The mistress of Tom Buchanan, a working-class woman trapped in an unhappy marriage.
- **George Wilson**: Myrtle's husband, a simple, hardworking gas station owner.

## Plot Summary

### Setting the Scene
Nick Carraway moves to West Egg, Long Island, and rents a small house next to the mysterious millionaire Jay Gatsby. Nick's cousin Daisy lives across the bay in the fashionable East Egg with her wealthy but crude husband, Tom Buchanan. Nick attends one of Gatsby's extravagant, lavish parties, where he meets Jordan Baker and learns about the mysterious host.

### The Reunion
Nick facilitates a reunion between Gatsby and Daisy, who had been lovers years before but separated due to Gatsby's poverty. Gatsby has built his fortune specifically to win Daisy back. Daisy is charmed but conflicted between her affection for Gatsby and her loyalty to Tom.

### Growing Tension
Tom becomes suspicious and confrontational. A group visits a hotel, where Tom openly questions Gatsby's sources of wealth and accuses him of criminal activities. Gatsby insists he only did what was necessary to obtain the money to win Daisy. Tom asserts that Gatsby cannot repeat the past, but Gatsby believes he can.

### The Tragedy
On the drive back from the hotel, Gatsby's car (driven by Daisy, though Gatsby sits beside her) strikes and kills Myrtle Wilson. Tom, driving Myrtle's husband's car, stops to help but learns Gatsby was driving. George Wilson, heartbroken and enraged, seeks revenge, believing Gatsby is responsible.

Wilson shoots Gatsby as he floats in his pool and then shoots himself. Nick is devastated by the moral emptiness and carelessness of the wealthy elite who were responsible for the destruction.

## Themes

- **The American Dream**: The novel critiques the American Dream, showing how wealth and material success cannot guarantee happiness or authentic relationships.
- **Class and Social Inequality**: The distinction between old money (East Egg) and new money (West Egg) highlights persistent class divisions and prejudices.
- **Illusion vs. Reality**: Gatsby's dream of recapturing the past is an illusion; the past cannot be relived, and appearances mask emptiness.
- **Morality and Decay**: Beneath the glamour of the Jazz Age lies moral decay, infidelity, and carelessness among the wealthy.
- **Nostalgia and the Past**: The novel explores the human desire to return to the past and the impossibility of doing so.

## Style and Significance

Fitzgerald's prose is elegant and carefully crafted, with rich symbolism. The green light at the end of Daisy's dock symbolizes Gatsby's dreams and their ultimate unreachability. The novel's critique of American society and its exploration of timeless themes—love, ambition, class—have made it enduringly relevant.

## Conclusion

Gatsby's death marks the tragic end of his dream. Nick reflects that Gatsby believed in the green light, in the future, even though that future was already past. The novel concludes with Nick's meditation on how we are all "boats beating against the current, borne back ceaselessly into the past." The tragedy serves as a commentary on the destructive nature of obsession and the illusions that drive human ambition.

## Literary Legacy

*The Great Gatsby* is considered essential American literature and is frequently taught in schools. Its exploration of the American Dream, class dynamics, and moral ambiguity continues to resonate with readers. The novel's innovative narrative technique and beautiful prose have cemented its place as one of the greatest works of fiction ever written.
