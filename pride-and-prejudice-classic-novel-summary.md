# Robin Hood — Classic Novel Summary

Robin Hood is a legendary English folk hero whose stories have been recounted in ballads, poems, and novels for centuries. The most famous novel adaptation is Howard Pyle’s The Merry Adventures of Robin Hood (1883), which popularized the character in modern times.

Set in medieval England, the tales follow Robin Hood, an agile archer and skilled swordsman, who becomes an outlaw after rebelling against the injustices of Sheriff of Nottingham and corrupt ruling elites. With his band of "Merry Men"—including figures like Little John, Friar Tuck, and Maid Marian—Robin Hood lives in Sherwood Forest. He famously robs from the rich to give to the poor, championing fairness, generosity, and resistance against tyranny.

The stories cover clever escapades, daring rescues, and close friendships, often focused on the struggle for justice and the redistribution of wealth. Robin Hood’s unwavering moral code, bravery, and compassion make him an enduring symbol of heroism and hope for oppressed people. The legend concludes with Robin’s eventual pardon by King Richard the Lionheart, restoring justice and peace.
