# The Picture of Dorian Gray - Novel Summary

**Author:** Oscar Wilde  
**Published:** 1890  
**Genre:** Gothic Fiction, Philosophical Novel  

## Overview

"The Picture of Dorian Gray" is Oscar Wilde's only novel, a dark tale that explores themes of aestheticism, moral corruption, and the pursuit of eternal youth and beauty. The story serves as both a critique of Victorian society and a meditation on art, life, and the consequences of hedonistic living.

## Plot Summary

The novel centers on Dorian Gray, a beautiful young man whose portrait is painted by the artist Basil Hallward. Under the influence of the hedonistic Lord Henry Wotton, Dorian becomes obsessed with his own beauty and youth. In a moment of vanity, he wishes that the portrait would age instead of him, allowing him to remain forever young.

His wish is mysteriously granted. As Dorian indulges in a life of pleasure, vice, and moral corruption over the following years, his physical appearance remains unchanged while the portrait bears the marks of his sins and aging. The painting becomes increasingly hideous, reflecting his moral decay.

The story follows Dorian's descent into depravity, including his influence on others that leads to their ruin and his indirect responsibility for several deaths, including that of Basil Hallward, whom he murders when the artist discovers the secret of the portrait.

## Key Characters

- **Dorian Gray**: The protagonist, a young man of extraordinary beauty who becomes corrupted by his own vanity and Lord Henry's influence
- **Lord Henry Wotton**: A witty, cynical aristocrat who introduces Dorian to a philosophy of hedonism and aestheticism
- **Basil Hallward**: The artist who paints Dorian's portrait and represents moral conscience in the novel
- **Sibyl Vane**: A young actress who falls in love with Dorian, whose tragic fate marks Dorian's first major moral failing

## Major Themes

### Art and Life
Wilde explores the relationship between art and life, questioning whether life should imitate art or vice versa. The portrait serves as a supernatural manifestation of this theme.

### The Pursuit of Beauty and Pleasure
The novel critiques the aesthetic movement's emphasis on beauty and pleasure above moral considerations, showing the destructive consequences of such a philosophy.

### Moral Corruption and Consequence
Despite the supernatural elements, the story demonstrates that moral corruption inevitably leads to spiritual and ultimately physical destruction.

### Youth and Aging
The fear of aging and loss of beauty drives much of the plot, reflecting Victorian anxieties about mortality and the passage of time.

## Literary Significance

"The Picture of Dorian Gray" stands as one of the most important works of aesthetic literature and a masterpiece of Gothic fiction. Wilde's wit and paradoxical epigrams throughout the text provide sharp social commentary on Victorian hypocrisy and values.

The novel was controversial upon publication, with critics condemning its perceived immorality. This controversy would later be used as evidence against Wilde during his famous trials for "gross indecency."

## Legacy

The book has become a classic examination of vanity, moral responsibility, and the dangers of living purely for aesthetic pleasure. Its influence can be seen in countless adaptations and works that explore similar themes of corruption hidden beneath beautiful surfaces.

The character of Dorian Gray has become archetypal, representing the dark side of eternal youth and beauty, while the concept of a portrait that ages instead of its subject has entered popular culture as a powerful metaphor for hidden moral decay.
