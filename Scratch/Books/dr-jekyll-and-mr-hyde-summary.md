# The Strange Case of Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde - Novel Summary

**Author:** Robert Louis Stevenson  
**Published:** 1886  
**Genre:** Gothic Fiction, Psychological Thriller, Horror  
**Setting:** Victorian London

## Plot Summary

**The Strange Case of Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde** is a psychological thriller exploring the duality of human nature. The story unfolds through the perspective of Mr. Gabriel John Utterson, a London lawyer and friend of the respected Dr. Henry Jekyll.

The tale begins when Utterson hears a disturbing story from his cousin Richard Enfield about a man named Edward Hyde who trampled a young girl and showed no remorse. Hyde paid compensation to avoid scandal, using a check signed by the well-respected Dr. Jekyll. This connection troubles Utterson, especially when he discovers that Jekyll's will leaves everything to Hyde.

Utterson becomes increasingly concerned about Jekyll's association with the sinister Hyde, whom he describes as giving an impression of "deformity without any nameable malformation." When Hyde murders a prominent MP, Sir Danvers Carew, Jekyll seems to end his association with Hyde, becoming more social and active in charitable works.

However, Jekyll soon becomes reclusive again, refusing to see friends including Utterson. Dr. Lanyon, Jekyll's colleague, becomes ill and dies after witnessing something that horrified him involving Jekyll and Hyde. Before dying, Lanyon gives Utterson a letter to be opened only after Jekyll's death or disappearance.

The climax comes when Jekyll's butler, Poole, asks Utterson to help break into Jekyll's laboratory. They find Hyde's body, apparently having committed suicide, and no trace of Jekyll. Utterson then reads both Lanyon's letter and Jekyll's full confession, which reveals the shocking truth: Jekyll and Hyde are the same person.

Jekyll had created a chemical formula that allowed him to transform into Hyde, initially as a way to indulge his darker impulses without consequences. However, the transformations became involuntary, and Hyde's evil nature grew stronger, eventually taking over completely. The final confession ends as Jekyll feels the final transformation beginning, knowing he will become Hyde forever.

## Key Characters

- **Dr. Henry Jekyll**: Respected physician and scientist who creates a potion to separate his good and evil natures
- **Mr. Edward Hyde**: Jekyll's evil alter ego, described as smaller, younger, and inherently repulsive
- **Mr. Gabriel John Utterson**: Lawyer and Jekyll's friend who investigates the mystery
- **Dr. Hastie Lanyon**: Jekyll's colleague who discovers the truth and dies from the shock
- **Richard Enfield**: Utterson's cousin who first tells the story of Hyde's cruelty
- **Poole**: Jekyll's loyal butler who helps Utterson discover the truth

## Major Themes

**The Duality of Human Nature**: The central theme explores how every person contains both good and evil impulses, and the danger of trying to separate them.

**Victorian Morality and Hypocrisy**: The novella critiques the restrictive moral codes of Victorian society that forced people to repress natural desires.

**Science vs. Nature**: Jekyll's scientific experiment represents the dangerous consequences of trying to control or manipulate human nature.

**Reputation and Social Status**: The characters are deeply concerned with maintaining their social standing, reflecting Victorian values.

**The Unconscious Mind**: The story predates Freudian psychology but explores similar concepts of repressed desires and the unconscious.

## Literary Significance

**Psychological Realism**: One of the first works to explore psychological complexity and the concept of split personality in literature.

**Gothic Tradition**: Continues the Gothic tradition with its dark urban setting, supernatural elements, and psychological horror.

**Influence on Popular Culture**: The phrase "Jekyll and Hyde" has become synonymous with split personality or dual nature.

**Narrative Structure**: The mystery unfolds through multiple perspectives and documents, creating suspense and psychological depth.

## Cultural Impact

The novella has had an enormous influence on literature, psychology, and popular culture. It's been adapted numerous times for stage, film, and television. The story resonates with modern audiences dealing with questions of identity, morality, and the hidden aspects of personality. It also prefigures later psychological theories about the unconscious mind and multiple personalities.

The work remains relevant in discussions about human nature, moral responsibility, and the consequences of scientific experimentation on the human condition.
