Hey Josh,

I wanted to take some time to write out my thoughts, because I thought it might be helpful for you to see them in writing.

First, I want you to know that I am supportive of you taking appropriate action to preserve your rights with the children. From what I understand, Judith preventing you from visiting with them or talking with them does not seem justified given the circumstances. But I would strongly urge caution about taking things further than that right now.

Maybe you can help me understand why you feel so compelled to press the business issues immediately. I agree that Judith could communicate with you better. I understand that. And I understand your desire to have more control over your own money. Given that you are separated, that seems very reasonable. But I do not think you need to split up the business in order to take a salary or move money into your own accounts. It seems to me that you could simply inform Judith that you are going to do so.

Going beyond that right now seems likely to invite more harm.

I also think your impulse to get the elders involved is right. But I would urge you to follow through on that fully. How can you expect your wife to submit to you if you are not also submitting yourself to the church?

“Likewise, you who are younger, be subject to the elders. Clothe yourselves, all of you, with humility toward one another, for ‘God opposes the proud but gives grace to the humble.’”\
1 Peter 5:5

I realize the elders have not acted as quickly as you desire. But elders can be slow to act on issues that require great care, especially when there are differences of opinion about how something should be handled. You need to give it time.

Part of submission really is being willing to wait. Waiting can be one of the ways we show that we are submitting ourselves to God.

From the outside, it looks to me like you may be jumping from step 3 to step 12 without doing the work to make sure that this is truly the only wise and faithful course of action.

I also have to say plainly that, in your situation, I do not see a biblical reason for divorce. It seems strange to invoke the business as a reason for it. I think 1 Corinthians 6 is very applicable here:

“When one of you has a grievance against another, does he dare go to law before the unrighteous instead of the saints? Or do you not know that the saints will judge the world? And if the world is to be judged by you, are you incompetent to try trivial cases? Do you not know that we are to judge angels? How much more, then, matters pertaining to this life! So if you have such cases, why do you lay them before those who have no standing in the church? I say this to your shame. Can it be that there is no one among you wise enough to settle a dispute between the brothers, but brother goes to law against brother, and that before unbelievers? To have lawsuits at all with one another is already a defeat for you. Why not rather suffer wrong? Why not rather be defrauded? But you yourselves wrong and defraud—even your own brothers!”\
1 Corinthians 6:1–8

You may be fed up with Judith, but you made vows to her, and those vows still mean something to God. Perhaps you do not actually intend to divorce, but only to threaten it. But even then, that is not a response of love. Threats usually come from fear and despair, not from faith, wisdom, or charity. In either case, I believe that if you pursue this path, you may regret it for the rest of your life.

Please play this out in your head.

Even in a better-case scenario, taking legal action will most likely make the situation far more difficult for you, your wife, and your children. It could result in the children being placed in other homes. And while Jenica and Alex may not be your first choice, there are worse places — much worse — where they could be placed by the state. At least right now, there is one parent who loves them and is nearby. In a worse-case scenario, you could lose access to the children completely, or they could even be harmed in foster care.

There are some lines that, once crossed, cannot be uncrossed. They can be forgiven, yes, but the consequences may live on. Tread carefully. Much of eternal importance may depend on how you carry yourself over the next few days and months.

Ask yourself why your closest counselors, your parents, and your friends are all recommending patience. Are all of us wrong? Your situation is serious, but I do not think it is so exceptional that you should ignore the counsel of those closest to you. You have other options.

“Without counsel plans fail, but with many advisers they succeed.”\
Proverbs 15:22

“Plans are established by counsel; by wise guidance wage war.”\
Proverbs 20:18

“My son, do not lose sight of these—keep sound wisdom and discretion, and they will be life for your soul and adornment for your neck. Then you will walk on your way securely, and your foot will not stumble.”\
Proverbs 3:21–23

I am saying all of this because I love you and want good for you, Judith, and the children. I am not trying to minimize the pain you are in. I just want to urge you, as strongly as I can, to slow down, listen to counsel, and not take steps that may be impossible to undo.

—John
